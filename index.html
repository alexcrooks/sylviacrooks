About the Author

Sylvia Crooks (née Shorthouse) was born and grew up in Nelson, BC. She is a graduate of the University of British Columbia, with a BA in English and History, and a Master's degree in Library Science. She worked in public libraries in Vancouver and Burnaby before joining the faculty of the UBC School of Library, Archival and Information Studies, where she taught reference and outreach services for 15 years before retiring in 2002. Her book Homefront & Battlefront: Nelson, BC in World War II, was given an honourable mention in 2005 by the BC Historical Federation. Names on a Cenotaph: Kootenay Lake Men in World War I was published in May, 2014

Books

Homefront & Battlefront: Nelson BC in World War II

Granville Island Publishing, 2005, 2007 (Rev. ed.)

During World War II, the small city of Nelson in southeastern British Columbia, sent 1300 of its 7,000 citizens off to war. Seventy did not come home. Homefront & Battlefront chronicles the lives and deaths of these courageous Canadians. It also tells the inspiring story of how the citizens of Nelson and surrounding communities came together to raise millions of dollars, send off thousands of pounds of clothing and tons of jam, to help make victory possible. It was called "the most patriotic town in BC."

Crooks has done such a good job, that even though she has concentrated on the war years in just one small B.C. Interior town, I can't think of a better book to give an understanding of what those years were like, anywhere in Canada.
Alan Woodland, Maple Ridge – Pitt Meadows Times
Homefront & Battlefront is like a precious scrapbook of our past...there's a universality to it that will create a resounding sense of déjà vu for any Canadian who experienced those days...It covers every aspect of the war years in a small community: challenges met, lives changed and lives lost. In these days of uncertainty and discontent, it reminds us of the way we were.
Norm Shannon, Esprit de Corps: Canadian Military
Homefront and Battlefront easily transcends the genre of local history and is a surprisingly compelling account of the lived experience of the war...A clear picture of Canada at war is revealed through the lens of a single community.
Paula Butler, BC Museums Roundup
In lucid prose, Crooks evokes the full range of emotions—hope, pride, dread, grief—that embrace a small community in the time of war. Many illustrations, and the words of the combatants themselves, lend immediacy to the story. This is about one town, but it could by Anytown, Canada, and so deserves a wide readership.
Basil Stuart-Stubbs, UBC Professor and University Librarian Emeritus

Names on a Cenotaph: Kootenay Lake Men in World War I

Granville Island Publishing, May 2014

On cenotaphs and memorials along the shores of beautiful Kootenay Lake in the British Columbia interior are the names of 280 men who died on the blood-soaked battlefields of World War I—amid the first deadly gas attack at Ypres, in the costly battle for Vimy Ridge and through the horrors of Passchendaele. They come alive again, often through their letters and diaries, in this account of who they were and what they endured in that cataclysmic "war to end all wars." After six years of research, the author tells their stories, and vividly makes them more than simply "names on a cenotaph."

Names on a Cenotaph is a remarkable achievement of rigorous research, thoughtful analysis and interpretation, and lively writing.
Sam McBride, author of "The Bravest Canadian: Fritz Peters, VC: The Making of a Hero in Two World Wars"
Names on a Cenotaph is a compelling and rewarding read.
Dr. Jean Barman, UBC Professor Emeritus and BC historian
The book will give the reader a greater understanding of the Great War, and a higher appreciation for the men who fought in it... It is a fascinating book, yet also disturbing as we see a steady stream of brave young men fall in badly. Sadly, that is what the war was all about.
Dave Obee, Editor-In-Chief, Times Colonist, Victoria
Sylvia Crooks puts faces to the names inscribed on the cenotaphs and memorials erected in memory of those 'Kootenay Lakers' who died during World War I. Her painstakingly researched narrative of their lives and families introduces a human touch to the cold statistics that record the war's horrific losses.
Lieutenant Colonel (Retired) David S. Leslie, CD, The Royal Canadian Regiment
